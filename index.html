Understanding Domestic Violence

What Is Domestic Violence?

Domestic violence (also called intimate partner violence, domestic abuse, or relationship abuse) is a pattern of behaviors used by one partner to maintain power and control over another partner in an intimate relationship.

Domestic violence does not discriminate. It affects people of all ages, economic backgrounds, education levels, races, religions, genders, and sexual orientations.

Forms of Abuse

  • Physical Abuse: Hitting, slapping, kicking, restraining, or any unwanted physical contact
  • Emotional/Psychological Abuse: Intimidation, manipulation, humiliation, isolation
  • Sexual Abuse: Any non-consensual sexual activity or coercion
  • Economic Abuse: Controlling finances, preventing employment, sabotaging work
  • Digital Abuse: Using technology to harass, stalk, or control
  • Coercive Control: Pattern of threatening, degrading, and controlling behaviors

Why This Resource Exists

This website exists to provide accessible, comprehensive, and life-saving information to anyone affected by domestic violence.

Whether you're experiencing abuse, supporting a loved one, or seeking to educate yourself, you'll find:

  • 24/7 crisis hotlines and emergency resources
  • Information about recognizing abuse patterns
  • Statistics and research to understand the scope
  • Local Idaho resources and national support services

Understanding the Power and Control Wheel

The Power and Control Wheel is a widely recognized tool that illustrates the pattern of abusive behaviors used by abusers to establish and maintain power and control over their partners. Click on each wheel to view it in detail.

Using Coercion and Threats

Making and/or carrying out threats to hurt; threatening to leave, commit suicide, report to welfare

Using Intimidation

Making her afraid by using looks, actions, gestures; smashing things; destroying property; abusing pets; displaying weapons

Using Emotional Abuse

Putting her down; making her feel bad about herself; calling her names; making her think she's crazy; playing mind games; humiliating her

Using Isolation

Controlling what she does, who she sees and talks to, what she reads, where she goes; limiting outside involvement; using jealousy to justify actions

Minimizing, Denying, and Blaming

Making light of the abuse and not taking concerns seriously; saying the abuse didn't happen; shifting responsibility for abusive behavior

Using Children

Making her feel guilty about the children; using children to relay messages; using visitation to harass; threatening to take children away

Using Male Privilege

Treating her like a servant; making all the big decisions; acting like the "master of the castle"; being the one to define roles

Using Economic Abuse

Preventing her from getting or keeping a job; making her ask for money; giving her an allowance; taking her money; not letting her know about family income

The Power and Control Wheel was developed by the Domestic Abuse Intervention Programs (DAIP) in Duluth, Minnesota. It illustrates the most common tactics used by abusive partners. While originally developed through the experiences of women in abusive relationships with men, these tactics are applicable to all intimate partner relationships.
