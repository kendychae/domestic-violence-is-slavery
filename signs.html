Recognizing Abuse: Signs & Symptoms

Understanding the warning signs can help you or someone you love get to safety

Is It Abuse?

Domestic violence doesn't always leave visible bruises. Abuse takes many forms—physical, emotional, sexual, financial, and digital. The core of all abuse is power and control: one partner using various tactics to dominate and manipulate the other.

If you're questioning whether your relationship is abusive, trust your instincts. You deserve to feel safe, respected, and valued in all your relationships.

Types of Abuse

Physical Abuse

Any intentional use of physical force with the intent to cause harm or fear.

Warning Signs:

  • Hitting, slapping, punching, kicking, or biting
  • Strangling or choking
  • Pushing, shoving, or grabbing
  • Using weapons to threaten or harm
  • Restraining you or preventing you from leaving
  • Throwing objects at you or near you
  • Driving recklessly to frighten you
  • Forcing you to use drugs or alcohol
  • Denying medical care or medication

Emotional & Psychological Abuse

Behaviors intended to undermine your self-worth and emotional wellbeing.

Warning Signs:

  • Constant criticism, insults, or name-calling
  • Humiliation in private or public
  • Blaming you for their abusive behavior
  • Gaslighting (making you doubt your reality)
  • Threatening to harm you, themselves, or loved ones
  • Threatening to take away children
  • Isolating you from family and friends
  • Monitoring your activities and whereabouts
  • Extreme jealousy or possessiveness
  • Destroying your belongings
  • Harming or threatening pets

Sexual Abuse

Any sexual activity without clear, voluntary consent.

Warning Signs:

  • Forcing or coercing sexual activity
  • Demanding sex when you're unwilling
  • Causing pain during sex deliberately
  • Refusing to use contraception or protection
  • Reproductive coercion (controlling birth control)
  • Sharing intimate images without consent
  • Making you perform sexual acts you're uncomfortable with
  • Attacking sexual parts of your body
  • Treating you as a sexual object

Economic & Financial Abuse

Controlling finances to create dependency and limit freedom.

Warning Signs:

  • Controlling all money and giving you an "allowance"
  • Preventing you from working or sabotaging your job
  • Stealing your money or refusing to contribute
  • Hiding financial information from you
  • Running up debt in your name
  • Refusing to let you access bank accounts
  • Forcing you to work without fair compensation
  • Making you account for every penny spent
  • Destroying your credit score

Digital Abuse

Using technology to harass, stalk, or control you.

Warning Signs:

  • Demanding passwords to your accounts
  • Constantly checking your phone, emails, or social media
  • Using GPS or apps to track your location
  • Sending threatening or harassing messages
  • Posting embarrassing information online
  • Sharing intimate images without permission
  • Impersonating you online
  • Controlling who you can contact online
  • Using spyware or monitoring software

Stalking

Repeated, unwanted attention that causes fear or concern for safety.

Warning Signs:

  • Following you or showing up uninvited
  • Watching your home, work, or school
  • Sending unwanted gifts or messages
  • Creating fake profiles to contact you
  • Gathering information through others
  • Making unwanted phone calls
  • Vandalizing your property
  • Threatening you or your loved ones

Coercive Control

A strategic pattern of behavior designed to make a victim dependent by isolating them, regulating their everyday activities, and limiting their resources.

Warning Signs:

  • Isolating you from friends, family, and support networks
  • Monitoring and controlling your daily activities and whereabouts
  • Controlling what you wear, eat, or how you spend your time
  • Regulating your access to basic necessities (food, sleep, medical care)
  • Micromanaging your behavior and decision-making
  • Making you feel like you need permission for everyday activities
  • Using rewards and punishments to control behavior
  • Gaslighting and manipulating your perception of reality
  • Creating an environment of fear and dependency
  • Making rules that only apply to you, not the abuser
  • Threatening consequences for not following demands
  • Gradually escalating control over time

Early Warning Signs: Red Flags

Abusive behavior often escalates over time. Recognizing early warning signs can help you make informed decisions about your safety.

Intense Relationship Progression

Rushing the relationship, wanting commitment very quickly, declaring love excessively early, or pressuring you to move in together.

Jealousy & Possessiveness

Extreme jealousy disguised as love, accusing you of cheating without reason, or getting angry when you spend time with others.

Controlling Behavior

Dictating what you wear, who you see, where you go, or how you spend your time. Needing to know your whereabouts at all times.

Isolation from Support System

Actively working to separate you from family and friends, criticizing your loved ones, or making it difficult to maintain relationships.

Blaming Others

Never taking responsibility for their actions, always blaming you or others for their problems, or making excuses for their behavior.

Explosive Temper

Unpredictable mood swings, overreacting to minor issues, rage that seems out of proportion, or "walking on eggshells" around them.

Disrespect

Putting you down, making fun of you, dismissing your feelings or opinions, or treating you as inferior.

Pressure for Sex

Not respecting your boundaries, pressuring you into sexual activity, or making you feel guilty for saying no.

Past Abusive Behavior

History of abusive relationships, blaming past partners for everything, or having a reputation for being violent.

Cruelty to Animals or Children

Harming, threatening, or showing callousness toward animals or children—strong predictor of violence toward humans.

Substance Abuse

Heavy drinking or drug use, particularly if it leads to aggressive or unpredictable behavior.

Threats of Violence

Any threat of physical harm toward you, themselves, or others should always be taken seriously.

Helping Someone You Care About

If you suspect someone you know is being abused, your support can make a life-saving difference.

Do:

  • Listen without judgment
  • Believe them and validate their feelings
  • Express concern for their safety
  • Offer specific help (childcare, transportation, etc.)
  • Help them create a safety plan
  • Provide resource information
  • Continue to stay in touch and show support
  • Respect their decisions and timeline
  • Call 911 if they're in immediate danger

Don't:

  • Pressure them to leave before they're ready
  • Judge or criticize their decisions
  • Give ultimatums
  • Confront the abuser
  • Blame the victim
  • Tell them what to do
  • Minimize the abuse or make excuses for the abuser
  • Share their situation without permission
  • Stop being there if they stay
